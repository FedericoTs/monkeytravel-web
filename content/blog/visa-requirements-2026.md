# Visa Requirements 2026: The Complete Country-by-Country Guide

Travel visas got more complex in 2026, not less. The European Union's long-delayed ETIAS authorization system finally launches this autumn, the United Kingdom now enforces its ETA requirement for nearly every non-British visitor, and at least eight countries that were previously visa-on-arrival have moved to mandatory e-visas applied for online before departure.

The good news: most of these new rules are paperwork, not refusals. The bad news: skip the paperwork and you will be turned away at the gate, with the airline absorbing a fine that they will gladly pass on to you.

This guide is the country-by-country reference we wish existed in a single place. We cover what changed in 2026, where you still need a real visa, where an e-visa or travel authorization is now required, and how to plan around the new systems before your trip.

> **Looking for a specific passport?** Our deep-dive for US passport holders is here: [Visa Requirements for US Citizens 2026](/blog/visa-requirements-us-citizens). For all other nationalities, the country-by-country tables below apply universally — but always confirm with the destination's official consulate page before you book.

## What changed in 2026 — the four big shifts

### 1. ETIAS launches in Europe (autumn 2026)

After three years of delays, the European Travel Information and Authorisation System (ETIAS) is going live in late 2026. It applies to visitors from approximately 60 visa-exempt countries — including the United States, Canada, Australia, the UK, Japan, South Korea, Brazil, Argentina, and Israel — when they enter any of the 30 Schengen Area countries plus Cyprus.

The mechanics are familiar to anyone who has done the US ESTA or Canada eTA:

- **Apply online** at the official `travel-europe.europa.eu` portal (no third-party sites; they all charge fees with no value added).
- **€7 fee** per application (free for under-18s and over-70s).
- **Approval typically within minutes**, but can take up to 30 days if your case requires manual review — apply at least three weeks before travel.
- **Valid for three years** (or until your passport expires), unlimited entries.
- **No physical document** — the authorization is linked to your passport in the EU database.

ETIAS is not a visa. It is a pre-screening that confirms you are eligible to enter the Schengen Area visa-free. Border officers still make the final call on entry.

### 2. UK ETA is fully enforced

The UK Electronic Travel Authorisation rolled out in waves through 2024 and 2025; in 2026 it is mandatory for nearly every non-British, non-Irish visitor who does not already hold a UK visa. That includes Americans, Canadians, Australians, EU citizens, Japanese, Singaporean, Brazilian, Mexican, and most other nationalities previously waved through with just a passport.

- **Apply online** at `gov.uk/eta` or the official UK ETA mobile app.
- **£16 fee** (~$20).
- **Approval within 72 hours** in most cases, sometimes minutes.
- **Valid for two years**, multiple entries of up to six months each.
- **Required for transit through London Heathrow even if you do not exit the airside zone** (one of the most common surprises that has caught travellers off-guard since the rule went live).

### 3. New e-visa requirements for eight previously-easy countries

Several destinations that used to issue visas on arrival have moved to mandatory online application in 2026. The shift is universal across nationalities (with some exceptions for neighbouring countries). If a destination below is on your itinerary, apply at least two weeks before departure:

- **Vietnam**: now an e-visa for 80+ nationalities, $25, 5–7 day processing
- **Sri Lanka**: ETA system, $35, near-instant for most passports
- **Indonesia**: e-VOA expansion, $35, instant for 90+ nationalities
- **Kenya**: e-Visa replaced visa-on-arrival in 2024, now strictly enforced, $51, 48-hour processing
- **Cambodia**: e-Visa, $36, 3–day processing
- **Tanzania**: e-Visa, $50–100 depending on passport, 10-day processing
- **India**: e-Visa for 160+ nationalities, $25–80 by duration, 3–5 day processing
- **Ethiopia**: e-Visa, $52 for 30-day single-entry, 24-hour processing

### 4. Schengen visa fee increased to €90

For the nationalities that still need a full Schengen visa (e.g., Indian, Chinese, Russian, South African, Nigerian passport holders), the standard short-stay visa fee rose to €90 in June 2024 and remained there into 2026 — a 12.5% increase from the previous €80. Children aged 6–12 pay €45; under-6s remain free.

## Common questions

**How early should I apply for an e-visa or travel authorization?**

For e-visas with a stated processing time, apply at least **double the stated processing time before departure**. So if a destination says 5 days, apply 10–14 days out. Processing systems get backlogged during high season (June–August northern hemisphere, December–January for Southeast Asia), and you do not want to be refreshing your inbox at the airport.

**What happens if my visa is denied?**

For most e-visas and travel authorizations, denials are rare but final — you cannot board the flight. You can reapply with corrected information, but the fee is non-refundable. For full visa applications (Schengen, US B1/B2, UK Standard Visitor), denials require either an appeal (rare to succeed) or a fresh application addressing the stated grounds. Always disclose previous denials on subsequent applications; lying about it is the single most common cause of permanent ineligibility.

**Do I need a visa to transit through a country?**

Often yes, even if you do not exit the airside. The UK ETA, US C-1 transit visa, and Chinese transit-without-visa rules are the most-asked questions in this space. The general rule: if you change terminals, pass through immigration, or stay over 24 hours, you need transit authorization. Some airports (Singapore Changi, Doha Hamad, Dubai DXB, Istanbul IST) are explicitly designed to let international transit passengers stay airside for up to 24 hours without entering the country.

**How long is my passport valid for travel?**

Most countries require your passport to be valid for **at least six months past your planned departure date** from the destination. This is the silent rejection: airlines will not let you board if your passport expires within that window, even if the destination would technically admit you. Renew at the 9-month mark if you travel internationally.

**Where can I check the official source?**

Always verify on the destination's **government immigration website** (search "[country name] official visa requirements"). The IATA Travel Centre (`iatatravelcentre.com`) is also a reliable aggregator. Avoid third-party services that charge "expedited processing" fees — they cannot speed up government processing and frequently overcharge for the same form you can fill in directly.

## How to plan a trip with new 2026 visa rules

The single biggest mistake travelers make in 2026 is planning the trip first and checking visas second. The new ETIAS, UK ETA, and country-specific e-visa systems mean you need to:

1. **Confirm visa requirements before booking flights** — a non-refundable ticket plus a 30-day visa processing time can mean you fly without the right document, and the airline refuses boarding.
2. **Apply for travel authorizations 3–4 weeks in advance**, even when the official processing time says 24–72 hours. Backlogs and document re-requests can extend that.
3. **Carry a printed copy of your authorization** in addition to the digital one. Some border officers still expect to see paper, and your phone battery has a way of dying at exactly the wrong moment.
