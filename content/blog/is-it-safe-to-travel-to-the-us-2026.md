# Is It Safe to Travel to the United States in 2026? What Every Traveler Needs to Know

The United States welcomed 75.7 million international visitors in 2019. By 2025, that number had dropped significantly, and 2026 is tracking even lower — with overseas travel to the US declining for nine consecutive months and counting.

The reasons are not a mystery. Expanded immigration enforcement, new visa fees, ICE agents deployed to airports, device searches at borders, social media screening proposals, and travel advisories issued by at least nine countries have changed the calculation for millions of would-be visitors.

This guide is not political commentary. It is a practical travel resource for anyone considering a trip to the United States in 2026 — what has actually changed, what the real risks are, what your rights are at the border, and how to make an informed decision about whether (and how) to visit.

---

## The Short Answer

**Yes, the United States remains broadly safe for international tourists in 2026.** Millions of visitors enter and leave the country without incident every month. The vast majority of travelers — particularly those from Visa Waiver Program countries with valid documentation — will have a normal experience.

**However**, the enforcement environment has materially changed, and certain categories of travelers face elevated risk of delays, enhanced scrutiny, or in rare cases, detention. The practical advice in this guide helps you understand which changes affect you and how to prepare.

## What Has Actually Changed in 2025-2026

### ICE Deployment to US Airports (March 2026)

In late March 2026, Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) deployed agents to at least 14 major US airports, including JFK (New York), LAX (Los Angeles), O'Hare (Chicago), Miami International, and Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International. This is a significant operational shift — historically, airports were the domain of Customs and Border Protection (CBP), not ICE.

**What this means for travelers:**
- ICE agents can question travelers about their immigration status at domestic airports, not just international entry points
- ICE's stated role is to support CBP operations, not to independently detain tourists — but the operational boundaries remain unclear in practice
- This primarily affects undocumented individuals and those with immigration violations, **not typical tourists or valid visa holders**
- However, the visible presence of immigration enforcement at airports has created anxiety for many travelers, including legal visitors
- The March 2026 partial DHS shutdown left some TSA officers working without pay, compounding airport disruptions and causing hours-long security lines at some hubs

**Practical impact for international tourists:** Low risk if your documentation is in order. But be prepared for the possibility of being asked about your immigration status at domestic terminals during connecting flights.

**Practical tip:** Carry printed copies of your visa, return flight confirmation, hotel booking, and travel insurance policy. Having physical documentation readily available can speed up any encounter with immigration officers.

**Note:** This situation is evolving rapidly. Travelers should check current conditions at their specific airports before flying, as ICE presence and its operational scope may change on short notice.

### Expanded Travel Ban (January 2026)

A presidential proclamation effective January 1, 2026 expanded travel restrictions to citizens of approximately 39 countries. This goes significantly beyond previous versions.

**Countries affected include:** Chad, Eritrea, Iran, Libya, North Korea, Somalia, Sudan, Syria, Venezuela, Yemen, and additional nations added under expanded vetting categories.

**What this means:** Citizens of affected countries are barred from obtaining most categories of US visas. Some exceptions exist for students and certain work visas, but tourist visas are broadly restricted. If you hold a passport from an affected country, check with the nearest US embassy before making any plans.

### DHS Funding Crisis (March 2026)

In March 2026, a partial shutdown of the Department of Homeland Security (DHS) left significant portions of the agency operating without full funding. The practical consequences for travelers have been immediate and disruptive.

**What happened:**
- TSA officers at some airports were required to work without pay during the funding gap
- Hours-long security lines were reported at major hubs including JFK, O'Hare, LAX, and Atlanta
- CBP staffing at ports of entry was reduced, leading to longer immigration processing times for arriving international passengers
- The situation has been fluid, with temporary funding extensions providing partial relief

**Practical advice for travelers:**
- **Arrive at least 3 hours early** for international flights departing from or connecting through major US airports
- **Consider enrolling in TSA PreCheck** ($78 for 5 years) or **Global Entry** ($100 for 5 years) to access expedited security and immigration lanes — these programs continued operating during the funding disruption
- **Monitor your airline's app** for real-time security wait time estimates at your departure airport
- **Build buffer time into connections** — if booking a connecting flight through a major US hub, allow at least 3 hours between flights rather than the usual 2

### $250 Visa Integrity and Border Security Fee (October 2025)

Signed into law in July 2025 and effective from October 2025, this fee applies to every nonimmigrant visa application — including tourist (B-1/B-2) visas. It is in addition to existing application fees.

**The math:** A couple applying for tourist visas now pays approximately $685 each ($185 application fee + $250 integrity fee + processing), or $1,370 total — before flights, hotels, or any travel spending. A family of four: $2,740 in visa fees alone.

**Impact:** This fee does not apply to Visa Waiver Program (ESTA) travelers from the 42 eligible countries. But for travelers from countries requiring visas, it represents a significant financial barrier.

### Enhanced Device Searches at Borders

CBP electronic device searches increased 17% in 2025 compared to 2024. The scope has expanded to include smartwatches and fitness trackers, not just phones and laptops.

**Your rights:**
- **US citizens and green card holders:** Cannot be denied entry for refusing a device search, but the device can be detained
- **Visa holders and ESTA travelers:** Can technically be denied entry for refusing, though this is uncommon for routine tourist travel
- CBP can conduct "basic searches" (scrolling through content) without suspicion. "Advanced searches" (connecting to forensic equipment) require reasonable suspicion

**Practical advice:** Back up your device before travel. Consider traveling with a clean device if you have concerns. Know that border agents may ask for passwords or PINs.

### Social Media Screening

The US has proposed mandatory social media disclosure for all ESTA (Visa Waiver Program) applicants, expected to take effect mid-2026. Currently, social media handles are collected on a voluntary basis on many visa application forms.

**What this means:** Content on public social media profiles may be reviewed as part of the entry screening process. There have been reports of travelers being questioned about social media posts at ports of entry.

## The "Trump Slump" in Numbers

The US tourism industry is experiencing its sharpest decline in international visitors outside of a pandemic.

**Key statistics:**
- **9 consecutive months** of declining international arrivals (through early 2026)
- **5.4% decline** in international visitors in 2025, with projections of **10%+ decline in 2026**
- **$30 billion+** in projected lost tourism revenue
- **14% drop** in European flight bookings to the US — even with the FIFA World Cup
- **62% of Canadians** say they are less likely to visit the US (YouGov survey)
- **$4.5 billion** estimated loss from the Canadian boycott alone
- Canada, Germany, Ireland, France, Switzerland, South Korea, and Argentina all showing significant declines in US-bound travel

**Why this matters for travelers:** Declining tourism can mean fewer flight routes, reduced service at tourist-oriented businesses, and a less welcoming atmosphere in some areas. On the flip side, lower demand could mean better hotel deals and shorter lines at major attractions.

## LGBTQ+ Traveler Safety

Several countries have specifically warned LGBTQ+ travelers — and particularly trans and nonbinary individuals — about visiting the United States.

### What Changed

- Federal executive orders defined sex as binary and based on biology, rolling back previous recognition of transgender identities at the federal level
- TSA screening procedures face uncertainty regarding gender-marker discrepancies on identification documents
- State-level protections vary dramatically — from strong protections in California, New York, and Illinois to restrictive policies in other states

### Practical Guidance

- **Major cities** (New York, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Chicago, Miami, Seattle, Portland, Boston) remain broadly welcoming with strong local protections
- **Documentation:** Ensure your passport gender marker matches your current presentation if possible, to minimize screening complications
- **Research your destination state:** LGBTQ+ safety varies significantly by state and region within the US
- **Organizations to check:** IGLTA (International LGBTQ+ Travel Association) maintains updated destination guides
- The UK, Canada, Germany, and several Nordic countries have specifically advised trans travelers to exercise additional caution

## FIFA World Cup 2026: The Visa Problem

The 2026 FIFA World Cup, co-hosted by the US, Canada, and Mexico (June 11 - July 19), has created a unique collision between global sports tourism and restrictive immigration policies.

### The Issue

- Fans from at least **4 qualified nations** — Haiti, Iran, Senegal, and Cote d'Ivoire — face severe visa restrictions or effective bans for entering the US
- A **$15,000 visa bond** requirement has been proposed for fans from certain countries, requiring a refundable deposit to guarantee departure
- The **$250 visa integrity fee** applies to all non-ESTA fan visa applications
- The expanded **travel ban** affects several countries whose teams qualified or could qualify

### What This Means for World Cup Travelers

- **ESTA-eligible countries** (most of Europe, Japan, South Korea, Australia, etc.): Straightforward entry, but apply early — processing times may increase due to volume
- **Visa-required countries:** Apply months in advance. Budget for $435-$685+ in visa fees per person
- **Banned countries:** Fans may need to attend matches held in Canada or Mexico instead
- **All travelers:** Expect enhanced security and longer processing times at US airports during the tournament

### Practical Tip

Consider attending World Cup matches hosted in **Canada or Mexico** if visa complications make US entry difficult. Both countries have more accessible entry requirements for most nationalities, and the tournament is spread across all three nations.

### FIFA World Cup 2026: Infrastructure and Planning Considerations

The 2026 FIFA World Cup runs from June 11 to July 19 across 16 host cities in the United States, Canada, and Mexico. With over 5 million visitors expected, the tournament will place extraordinary strain on travel infrastructure.

**Key considerations:**
- **Scale:** This is the largest World Cup in history (48 teams, 104 matches). US host cities include New York/New Jersey, Los Angeles, Dallas, Houston, Atlanta, Miami, Philadelphia, Seattle, San Francisco, Boston, and Kansas City
- **Airport capacity:** Major airports in host cities will face significantly higher passenger volumes during the tournament. Expect longer check-in, security, and immigration lines
- **Special visa processing:** FIFA and US authorities may announce expedited visa processing for confirmed ticket holders — check [FIFA's official travel page](https://www.fifa.com/fifaplus/en/tournaments/mens/worldcup/canadamexicousa2026) for updates
- **Accommodation:** Hotel prices in host cities are expected to surge 200-400% during match days. Book early or consider staying in nearby cities and commuting to matches

## Your Rights at the US Border

Understanding your rights helps you navigate border encounters confidently.

### If You Are a Tourist or Visa Holder

- **You must present valid documentation** (passport, visa or ESTA, return ticket)
- **CBP officers have broad authority** to question you about the purpose of your visit, your itinerary, and your finances
- **You can be denied entry** at the officer's discretion, even with valid documentation (though this is uncommon for prepared travelers)
- **You are not required to disclose passwords** to social media accounts, but refusal may result in additional questioning or, in rare cases, denial of entry
- **If detained:** You have the right to contact your country's embassy or consulate. Request this immediately.

### If You Are a Green Card Holder

- **You have strong re-entry rights** — you cannot be casually turned away
- **However:** Green card holders have been detained in 2025-2026 in cases involving alleged immigration violations, criminal history, or political activity
- **Carry your green card and passport** at all times during travel
- **Consult an immigration attorney** before traveling if you have any concerns about your status

### If You Are an ESTA/Visa Waiver Traveler

- **ESTA approval does not guarantee entry** — it authorizes travel to a US port of entry, where CBP makes the final decision
- **Be prepared to show:** Return ticket, hotel reservations, proof of funds, and your travel itinerary
- **Keep your story simple and consistent** — answer questions truthfully and concisely about the purpose of your visit

### Universal Tips for All Travelers

1. **Carry printed copies** of your visa, ESTA approval, hotel bookings, and return flights
2. **Know your embassy's emergency number** and save it in your phone
3. **Do not volunteer information** — answer questions asked, but do not over-explain
4. **Remain calm and polite** — confrontational behavior escalates situations
5. **If secondary screening occurs:** This is standard procedure for many travelers and does not mean you are in trouble

## Practical Preparation Checklist

Before traveling to the United States in 2026:

- [ ] **Check your country's travel advisory** for the US on your foreign ministry's website
- [ ] **Verify your visa/ESTA status** — apply well in advance (ESTA: 72+ hours; visa: 4-8 weeks minimum)
- [ ] **Ensure passport validity** — at least 6 months beyond your planned departure from the US
- [ ] **Backup your phone** — photos, contacts, messages. Consider traveling with a clean device
- [ ] **Review your social media** — remove or privatize content that could be misinterpreted during screening
- [ ] **Print key documents** — visa approval, ESTA confirmation, hotel reservations, return flights, travel insurance
- [ ] **Purchase travel insurance** — including coverage for trip interruption and medical emergencies (US healthcare is expensive)
- [ ] **Save your embassy's contact information** — both the main number and after-hours emergency line
- [ ] **Research your specific destination** — safety, local laws, and cultural norms vary significantly across the US
- [ ] **Carry proof of funds** — bank statements or credit card with sufficient balance for your stay
- [ ] **Know the 90-day limit** — ESTA and most tourist visas allow a maximum 90-day stay

## Should You Cancel Your US Trip?

**Not necessarily.** Here is a decision framework:

### Go ahead if:
- You hold a passport from a Visa Waiver Program (ESTA) country
- Your documentation is fully in order
- You are visiting major tourist destinations (NYC, LA, national parks, Florida)
- You are comfortable with the possibility of enhanced screening
- You have travel insurance

### Exercise additional caution if:
- Your country has issued a specific travel advisory about the US
- You require a visa (not ESTA) — factor in the $250 additional fee and longer processing times
- You are a trans or nonbinary traveler, particularly if your documentation has gender-marker discrepancies
- You have previously been denied entry to any country or have immigration history complications
- You plan to visit for political, academic, or activist purposes

### Consider alternatives if:
- Your country is on the expanded travel ban list
- You are unable to obtain a US visa
- The combined cost of visa fees ($435-$685 per person) makes the trip financially impractical
- You are not comfortable with device searches or social media screening

## The US Is Big — Safety Varies by Location

It is important to remember that the United States is a continent-sized country with enormous regional variation. Generalizing about "the US" is about as useful as generalizing about "Europe."

- **Major cities** (New York, San Francisco, Chicago, Boston, Seattle) tend to be diverse, cosmopolitan, and welcoming to international visitors
- **Tourist destinations** (national parks, theme parks, beach resorts) are specifically designed around visitor hospitality
- **University towns** are typically open and internationally minded
- **Rural areas** vary significantly — many are friendly and safe, but cultural norms differ from cities
- **Border regions** (within 100 miles of any US border) may have additional CBP checkpoints on highways

**The best approach:** Research your specific destination rather than making decisions based on national-level news coverage.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the US more dangerous for tourists in 2026 than previous years?

For most tourists with valid documentation, the physical safety risk has not meaningfully changed. The US remains a country with both safe and unsafe areas, as it has always been. What has changed is the **immigration enforcement environment** — the process of entering the country involves more scrutiny, higher fees, and the possibility of enhanced screening. The risk is primarily administrative, not physical.

### Can I be denied entry to the US even with a valid visa?

Yes. A US visa grants permission to travel to a port of entry, where a CBP officer makes the final admission decision. Denial with a valid visa is uncommon but possible. Common reasons include: insufficient proof of ties to your home country, inability to demonstrate sufficient funds, prior immigration violations, or inconsistencies between your stated purpose and documentation.

### Should I be worried about ICE at airports?

If you are a tourist with valid documentation, ICE airport operations are unlikely to affect you. The deployment primarily targets individuals with immigration violations. However, be prepared for the possibility of being asked about your immigration status during domestic connections.

### How does the $250 visa fee affect Visa Waiver Program (ESTA) travelers?

It does not. The $250 Visa Integrity and Border Security Fee applies only to nonimmigrant visa applications processed through US embassies and consulates. ESTA travelers from the 42 Visa Waiver Program countries pay the standard $21 ESTA fee only.

### Is it safe for LGBTQ+ travelers to visit the US?

Major US cities remain welcoming to LGBTQ+ travelers, with strong local anti-discrimination protections. The risk is primarily for trans and nonbinary travelers, particularly regarding documentation discrepancies during screening. Research your specific destination state, as protections vary dramatically.

### Will the situation change after the World Cup?

Immigration and visa policies are set by executive order and legislation, not event schedules. While there may be temporary processing accommodations for World Cup ticket holders, the underlying enforcement environment is unlikely to change significantly around the tournament.

### What happens if I'm detained at the border?

Request to contact your country's embassy or consulate immediately. You have this right under international law (the Vienna Convention on Consular Relations). Do not sign any documents you do not understand. If possible, have the contact information for an immigration attorney available.
