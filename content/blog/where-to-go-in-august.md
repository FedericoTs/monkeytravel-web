# Where to Go in August 2026: 12 Best Destinations for Smart Summer Travel

August is the month when most of the world travels at once — and pays for it. Mediterranean resorts hit their annual peak: overpriced, overheated, and overbooked. The smart move is not to avoid traveling in August. It is to travel to the right places.

Some destinations are objectively better in August than any other month. Edinburgh transforms into the world's biggest arts festival. Tanzania's Serengeti reaches peak river-crossing season for the Great Migration. Norway's fjords offer long, warm days and surprisingly light crowds. And in the Southern Hemisphere, August is winter — which means skiing in Patagonia, whale watching off South Africa, and dry-season wildlife viewing that outperforms anything the Northern Hemisphere summer can offer.

We picked 12 destinations that specifically reward August travel. For each one, you get real weather data, honest daily budgets (accommodation, food, transport, and one activity per day — flights excluded since they vary by origin), crowd assessments, and the August-specific events or conditions that make it the right month to book.

## Festival Destinations

August is the peak month for some of the world's most significant cultural events. These are destinations where August is not just acceptable — it is the only month worth visiting.

### 1. Edinburgh, Scotland

**Why August:** The Edinburgh Festival Fringe is the largest arts festival on the planet. For all of August, the city hosts over 3,400 shows across 300+ venues — comedy, theater, dance, circus, spoken word, and experimental performance from 60+ countries. Running simultaneously: the Edinburgh International Festival (classical music, opera, theater), the Military Tattoo at the Castle, and the Art, Book, and Film Festivals. Edinburgh in August is a city that has lost its mind in the best possible way.

**Weather:** Highs of 19°C (66°F), lows of 12°C (55°F). Scotland in August is not warm, but it is reliably dry by Scottish standards — about 12 rainy days, usually short showers rather than all-day downpours. Pack layers and a waterproof jacket. The long northern daylight (sunset after 9 PM) more than compensates.

**What to do:** Catch three or four Fringe shows per day — tickets range from free to $30, with the best comedy and drama selling out weeks ahead. Line up for the Royal Mile street performers. Take a morning hike up Arthur's Seat (the volcanic peak in Holyrood Park, free, 45 minutes) before the festival crowds wake up. Attend the Edinburgh International Festival's main stage events at the Usher Hall or Festival Theatre. Watch the Military Tattoo against the Castle backdrop if you can get tickets (book months ahead, $50-130).

**Daily budget:** $130-200 per person. August is Edinburgh's most expensive month. Budget accommodation books out quickly — hostels run $35-60/night and hotels from $120/night. Book accommodation six months ahead. The festival itself can be experienced cheaply: dozens of free shows daily, and full Fringe tickets average $15-20. A pub lunch costs $12-16; Edinburgh's whisky bars charge $7-15 per dram.

**Insider tip:** The "Free Fringe" — where performers pass a bucket at the end instead of charging upfront — includes some of the festival's best acts. Check PBH's Free Fringe and Laughing Horse listings. You can do three or four excellent shows in a day without spending a penny on tickets.

### 2. Kyoto, Japan

**Why August:** [Kyoto](/destinations/tokyo) in August centers on Obon, the Buddhist festival honoring ancestors' spirits — one of Japan's most atmospheric and culturally significant events. The Gozan Okuribi (August 16) illuminates five mountain kanji with enormous bonfires visible across the city as families guide ancestral spirits back to the afterlife. Gion Matsuri extends into August's first half. Traditional Bon Odori folk dances take place in temple grounds across the city every evening. Yes, August is hot. That is part of the deal — early mornings and evenings reveal a Kyoto that feels genuinely different from any other month.

**Weather:** Highs of 35°C (95°F), lows of 25°C (77°F). August is Kyoto's hottest and most humid month — genuinely intense. Plan outdoor sightseeing for before 9 AM and after 5 PM. The heat drives down prices and tourists compared to spring cherry blossom season.

**What to do:** Watch the Gozan Okuribi fires from a rooftop or hillside on August 16 — arrive two hours early for a good spot. Attend Bon Odori dances at Yasaka Shrine in the Gion district (free, evenings). Visit Arashiyama's bamboo grove at 6 AM before the heat builds. Take the Sagano Scenic Railway for mountain views and cool forest air. Day-trip to Nara for the deer and Todai-ji temple. Eat kaiseki dinner at a restaurant overlooking the Kamo River on a yuka (outdoor riverside platform).

**Daily budget:** $110-170 per person. August is cheaper than spring or autumn in Kyoto — hotels drop 20-30% from peak sakura pricing. A budget guesthouse or capsule hotel runs $40-70/night. A ramen lunch costs $8-12. Shrine entry is free or $5-8. The city is navigable by bus ($2 per ride) or bicycle ($10-15/day rental).

**Insider tip:** Book a ryokan (traditional inn) with a private onsen bath for at least one night. The outdoor bath in the predawn hours, when the city is silent and the air is finally cool, is one of travel's great sensory experiences. Prices for a ryokan with breakfast and dinner range from $80-200 per person.

## Hidden Gems and Smart Alternatives

### 3. Slovenia (Lake Bled and Beyond)

**Why August:** Slovenia is what people are really looking for when they book Croatia in August but find it overrun. Lake Bled — a glacial lake with a church-topped island and a clifftop castle — is postcard-perfect, and while it draws crowds, it is nowhere near the saturation levels of Dubrovnik or Santorini. Triglav National Park offers serious alpine hiking. The Soča Valley delivers piercing turquoise river water for kayaking, canyoning, and swimming. And Ljubljana, one of Europe's smallest and most charming capitals, functions as a civilized base.

**Weather:** Highs of 26°C (79°F), lows of 14°C (59°F). August is Slovenia's warmest and sunniest month. The lake water reaches 22°C — genuinely warm for alpine swimming. Afternoon thunderstorms are possible in the mountains; plan hikes for mornings.

**What to do:** Row out to the island church on Lake Bled (rowboat hire $20-25/hour) and ring the bell for good luck. Hike up to Bled Castle for the best view of the lake ($15). Trek or take a shuttle to Vintgar Gorge — a 1.6km canyon boardwalk above rushing turquoise water ($10). Rent a car and drive to the Soča Valley for swimming and canyoning. Visit Predjama Castle, a medieval fortress built into a cave mouth.

**Daily budget:** $90-140 per person. Slovenia is excellent value by Western European standards. A pension near Lake Bled costs $60-90/night. A cream cake (kremšnita) at the Park Hotel Café is an $5 institution. A full dinner in Ljubljana runs $15-25. Buses between towns cost $4-10.

**Insider tip:** Stay in the village of Ribčev Laz on the lake's eastern shore rather than the town of Bled — better views, quieter atmosphere, 15-20% cheaper. Visit the lake before 8 AM when the reflections are perfect and the tour groups haven't arrived.

### 4. Montenegro

**Why August:** Montenegro is what Dubrovnik used to be before the cruise ships arrived: dramatic medieval walls, pristine Adriatic bays, and a pace of life that hasn't been fully commodified yet. Kotor's UNESCO-listed Old Town is 30 minutes from Dubrovnik airport and a fraction of the crowds. The Bay of Kotor (often called Europe's southernmost fjord) offers a setting as dramatic as Croatia's coast. And prices are significantly lower — Montenegro is not yet in the EU, which keeps costs at a level that feels like Croatia from ten years ago.

**Weather:** Highs of 30°C (86°F), lows of 20°C (68°F). The Adriatic reaches 27°C — bathtub warm. August is one of Montenegro's driest months. Coastal areas are reliably sunny; the mountains behind Kotor catch afternoon cloud.

**What to do:** Walk Kotor's city walls to the fortress above (1,350 steps, $8 entry, incredible views). Take a boat to the tiny artificial island of Our Lady of the Rocks for its baroque church. Drive to Sveti Stefan, the pink-roofed fortified island village that has become Montenegro's most photographed image. Hike from Kotor up to the Vrmac ridge for a view across the entire bay. Take a day trip to Durmitor National Park for mountain scenery and the Tara River Canyon.

**Daily budget:** $80-130 per person. Montenegro punches well above its weight for value. A room in a Kotor Old Town guesthouse costs $50-80/night. A grilled fish dinner at a harbor restaurant runs $15-25. A beer is $2. Boat taxis across the bay cost $2-5.

**Insider tip:** The Kotor city walls are best climbed at 7 AM before the August heat makes them grueling. Bring water, and budget 90 minutes for the full ascent to the fortress. The view from the top over the labyrinthine bay at sunrise is worth the early alarm.

## Adventure and Nature

### 5. Canadian Rockies (Banff and Jasper)

**Why August:** August adds something that July cannot fully deliver in the Canadian Rockies: complete trail access and settled weather. The highest passes and alpine meadows are snow-free, wildlife is at maximum activity, and the parks operate at full capacity with every cable car, boat rental, and trail open. The Icefields Parkway between Banff and Jasper — often called the most scenic drive in the world — is at its absolute best. And unlike July, August evenings occasionally display the Northern Lights as days shorten.

**Weather:** Highs of 23°C (73°F), lows of 8°C (46°F). August is statistically the warmest and driest month in the Rockies. Mountain weather remains unpredictable — pack layers regardless. Sunset comes earlier than July (around 8:30 PM), which means the Northern Lights are occasionally visible on clear nights.

**What to do:** Hike the Plain of Six Glaciers from Lake Louise — 10.6km return with views of Victoria Glacier ($3 tea house at the turnaround point). Canoe on Moraine Lake at sunrise before the parking restrictions kick in (arrive before 6 AM or take the Parks Canada shuttle). Walk the Columbia Icefield and take the Ice Explorer onto the Athabasca Glacier ($60-70). Wildlife-watch along the Bow Valley Parkway at dusk for bears, moose, and wolves. Soak in Banff's Upper Hot Springs after a long hike ($10).

**Daily budget:** $150-250 per person. August pricing in Banff is peak — hotels in town run $200+/night. Camping ($25-40/night via Parks Canada reservation) is the budget solution, but sites book out months in advance. Restaurant meals run $20-35. A Discovery Pass ($73 CAD for the year) covers all national park entry and pays for itself immediately.

**Insider tip:** Jasper, 90 minutes north of Banff along the Icefields Parkway, has the same mountain scenery with noticeably fewer crowds and lower accommodation prices. The Dark Sky Preserve designation means Jasper offers Canada's best stargazing — and August's darker evenings make it ideal.

### 6. Norway Fjords

**Why August:** Norway's fjord region hits its summer peak in August — warm enough for swimming in fjord inlets (15-18°C, bracing but achievable), long days with 16+ hours of light, and wildflowers on the mountain passes. The village of Flam is the most famous gateway, but the real Norway lives along smaller fjords: Naeroyfjord, Hjorundfjord, and Hjelmelandsfjorden, where dramatic cliffs drop straight into still water and the only sounds are waterfalls. August is also when Norway's ferry network runs at maximum frequency, making island-hopping along the coast genuinely practical.

**Weather:** Highs of 20°C (68°F), lows of 11°C (52°F). August is one of Norway's warmer months, but temperatures vary significantly by latitude and elevation. Bergen is wetter than the inner fjords — expect some rain. Pack layers and a waterproof shell.

**What to do:** Take the Flam Railway — one of the steepest train journeys in the world, through mountain gorges and past waterfalls (50 minutes, $55 each way). Hike to Pulpit Rock (Preikestolen) for the 604m vertical drop view over Lysefjord — 8km return, allow 4 hours. Kayak on Naeroyfjord at dawn when the water is mirror-flat (guided tours from $80). Hike the Trolltunga trail — a 22km round trip to the tongue-shaped rock jutting over the valley ($130-180 with a guide). Cycle the Rallarvegen (Navvy Road) from Finse to Flam, 82km of mountain scenery.

**Daily budget:** $160-260 per person. Norway is genuinely expensive. Bergen hotels start at $130/night; guesthouses in smaller fjord villages run $80-120. Buying groceries at supermarkets cuts costs significantly — restaurant dinners run $35-55. The Norway in a Nutshell fjord tour ($170-200) is overpriced but convenient if you only have one day.

**Insider tip:** Skip the Norway in a Nutshell tourist circuit and build your own version. Buy individual Vy train and ferry tickets — it is cheaper, more flexible, and you avoid the guided group dynamic. The Rjukan-Notodden industrial heritage UNESCO site, often overlooked, is free and genuinely interesting.

### 7. Faroe Islands

**Why August:** August is the best month to visit the Faroe Islands, and it is still not crowded. The grass is at its most intensely green (the 18 islands receive so much rain that the color is almost surreal), the puffin colonies are at peak population before their September departure, and the weather — while never warm — is at its most stable. The Faroese landscape defies photography: vertical basalt cliffs over 700 meters high, sea stacks rising from black ocean, and villages of turf-roofed houses that look like they belong in a myth.

**Weather:** Highs of 13°C (55°F), lows of 9°C (48°F). The Faroe Islands are famously changeable — four seasons in one afternoon is a local cliche that happens to be accurate. Wind and rain are constant companions. Pack a serious waterproof outer layer and windproof fleece. But the dramatic low-cloud atmosphere is part of the visual appeal.

**What to do:** Hike to the Lake Sørvágsvatn optical illusion — the lake appears to float above the ocean (7km return from the village of Sørvágur, moderate). Walk the sea-cliff trail to Trælanípa, the "slave cliff." Visit the village of Gásadalur with its waterfall tumbling into the ocean. Hike Slættaratindur, the highest peak (882m), for a panorama over the entire archipelago. Take a ferry to the remote island of Mykines for puffin colonies (book ahead — the ferry fills up).

**Daily budget:** $170-250 per person. The Faroes are expensive but accessible. Guesthouses and farm stays run $90-140/night. Eating at the handful of restaurants costs $35-55 per meal; self-catering from the supermarket in Tórshavn is the budget play. Inter-island ferries are $8-25. No entry fees anywhere.

**Insider tip:** Rent a car (from $80/day) rather than relying on buses — the islands are small (total road network 500km) but public transport is infrequent. You can cover the main island of Streymoy and both side-islands in three days with a rental car. Book early: car rental supply is limited.

## Southern Hemisphere Winter Escapes

August is summer in the Northern Hemisphere but winter in the Southern Hemisphere — which creates some of the year's best travel opportunities for those willing to flip the script.

### 8. South Africa (Hermanus and the Cape)

**Why August:** Southern right whales return to Walker Bay near Hermanus every year between June and November, and August is peak season. Up to 200 whales breed and calf just offshore — visible from the clifftop cliff path with the naked eye. No boat needed. Hermanus has been called the world's best land-based whale watching destination. Beyond the whales, August is the Cape Winelands' winter: Stellenbosch and Franschhoek are quiet, unhurried, and still beautiful in the winter green. The Western Cape's fynbos biome flowers year-round. Penguin colonies at Boulders Beach are year-round residents.

**Weather:** Highs of 18°C (64°F), lows of 10°C (50°F). Cape Town in August can be wet and windy — the Cape of Good Hope earns its stormy reputation. But Hermanus and the Garden Route catch more sun. Pack for cold evenings and bring a raincoat; don't expect beach weather.

**What to do:** Walk the Hermanus Cliff Path for whale watching (free, 12km, allow 3-4 hours). Book a responsible whale watching boat trip for close approaches ($50-80). Visit the penguin colony at Boulders Beach near Simon's Town (R320 entry). Drive the Cape Winelands circuit: Stellenbosch, Franschhoek, and Paarl — wine tasting starts at $8-15 per estate. Hike Table Mountain via the Platteklip Gorge trail (free, 2 hours up) on a clear day.

**Daily budget:** $100-200 per person. The rand makes South Africa exceptional value for most international travelers. A guesthouse in Hermanus costs $60-100/night. A Cape Malay curry lunch in Bo-Kaap is $8-12. A mid-range restaurant dinner costs $20-30. A Stellenbosch wine tasting with cheese pairing runs $15-25.

**Insider tip:** The Hermanus Whale Festival runs in late September, but August actually offers better sightings with more whales present and zero festival crowds. Book accommodation in Hermanus town (walking distance to cliff path) rather than outlying self-catering — you want to walk down for spontaneous morning whale sightings.

### 9. Patagonia, Argentina (Bariloche and El Calafate)

**Why August:** August is Patagonian winter, which means skiing at Cerro Catedral — South America's largest ski resort, just outside Bariloche — and the frozen, stark beauty of Perito Moreno Glacier with no summer tourist queues. This is the contrarian Patagonia pick. Most travelers visit in November-February for trekking; visiting in August means half the accommodation cost, empty trails in the lower elevations, and the otherworldly experience of a glacier under winter skies. Bariloche itself — a Swiss-style chocolate-and-fondue mountain town on an Andean lake — is cozy and charming in winter.

**Weather:** Highs of 5°C (41°F), lows of -3°C (27°F) in Bariloche. Ski season conditions at Cerro Catedral: variable snow quality, can be excellent. El Calafate (for Perito Moreno Glacier): highs of 3°C (37°F), windy and cold. Pack serious winter gear — thermal layers, waterproof ski jacket, and insulated boots for the glacier.

**What to do:** Ski or snowboard at Cerro Catedral (day pass $50-70, full equipment rental $30-40). Take a boat across Nahuel Huapi Lake under snow-capped peaks. Eat Bariloche's famous chocolate at Del Turista or Rapa Nui. Drive or bus to El Calafate (1.5 hours from town) for a guided glacier trek on Perito Moreno ($100-150, includes crampons). Watch massive ice towers calve into Lago Argentino from the boardwalk (free viewing, $30 park entry).

**Daily budget:** $90-160 per person. Argentina's economic situation has historically made it good value for dollar and euro holders — confirm the current exchange rate before traveling. A hostel or B&B in Bariloche runs $30-60/night. A media-res (ribeye) at a parrilla costs $12-20. A day ski pass is $50-70.

**Insider tip:** Perito Moreno Glacier is one of the few advancing glaciers in the world — it calves dramatically every few years in a full rupture, but daily calving of ice towers is continuous. Book a "Big Ice" glacier trek ($150-180) rather than the standard minitrekking ($100) — you spend twice as long on the ice and reach sections most visitors never see.

## Monsoon Shoulder: Asia with Strategy

### 10. Indonesia (Komodo and Flores)

**Why August:** August sits squarely in Komodo's dry season — the best window for visiting the Komodo National Park. The sea is calm enough for diving and snorkeling, visibility in the water reaches 20-30 meters, and the Komodo dragons are active in the savannah heat. The pink sand beach at Pantai Merah, the manta ray cleaning stations at Manta Point, and the crystal-clear waters of Kanawa Island are all at their most accessible. Flores Island itself — the gateway — is dramatic volcanic country that most visitors rush through to get to Komodo, but deserves two or three days of its own.

**Weather:** Highs of 30°C (86°F), lows of 22°C (68°F). August is one of the driest months in Komodo. Ocean conditions are calm on most days, though the Komodo strait can get choppy — check sea conditions with operators before booking liveaboard trips.

**What to do:** Trek to see Komodo dragons at Rinca or Komodo Island with a mandatory ranger guide ($30-40 per person). Snorkel or dive at Manta Point for near-guaranteed manta ray encounters ($40-60 per dive). Hike Padar Island at sunrise for the three-bay panorama — one of Indonesia's most striking views. Swim at Pink Beach (Pantai Merah) in the afternoon. Take a 3-day liveaboard trip to cover more of the park ($150-350/night depending on boat quality).

**Daily budget:** $80-150 per person. The Komodo region is pricier than [Bali](/destinations/bali) due to boat transport and park fees ($25/day), but remains good value by global standards. A bungalow on Flores runs $25-50/night. Warung meals cost $3-6. The liveaboard option bundles accommodation, transport, and dives into one cost.

**Insider tip:** Flying into Labuan Bajo (Flores) rather than Bali and connecting has become significantly easier with direct flights from Bali and Jakarta. Spend two nights in Labuan Bajo, then three nights on a liveaboard — this gives you the dragons, the diving, and the sunrise hike without feeling rushed.

### 11. Costa Rica Pacific Coast (Green Season)

**Why August:** August is the heart of Costa Rica's green season on the Pacific coast — which sounds like a warning but is actually an opportunity. Prices drop 30-50% from peak season. The jungle is at its most lush. Turtle nesting peaks on Ostional Beach, where hundreds of olive ridley turtles come ashore in mass arrivals called arribadas. The surfing around Santa Teresa and Tamarindo is at its most powerful. And the wildlife — sloths, monkeys, jaguars, scarlet macaws — is more active and visible after rains. The trick: plan outdoor activities for mornings, when the coast is usually sunny, and accept that afternoons will bring brief (but heavy) showers.

**Weather:** Highs of 30°C (86°F), lows of 22°C (68°F). August sees significant afternoon rainfall — typically 2-4 hours of intense rain between 2-5 PM, then clearing. Mornings are almost always sunny. Humidity is high. The dry Pacific lowlands (Guanacaste) see less rain than the mountains.

**What to do:** Witness the olive ridley turtle arribada at Ostional Wildlife Refuge (nighttime, free, with local guide). Surf at Mal País and Santa Teresa — consistent 4-6 foot beach breaks. Take a boat tour in Manuel Antonio for sloth, monkey, and toucan sightings. White-water raft on the Naranjo River. Zip-line through the rainforest canopy above Monteverde Cloud Forest Reserve ($50-80). Soak in the hot springs at La Fortuna with Arenal Volcano looming overhead.

**Daily budget:** $100-160 per person. Green season pricing makes an otherwise pricey destination manageable. A jungle lodge that costs $200/night in December drops to $90-120 in August. A typical soda (local diner) meal runs $5-8. A guided wildlife tour is $40-70. Rental cars cost $40-60/day.

**Insider tip:** The turtle arribada at Ostional is one of the most dramatic wildlife events in the Americas, yet it is almost entirely undiscovered by mainstream tourism. Call the Ostional Development Association before your trip — they can give you real-time updates on when the next mass arrival is expected, since timing can shift by a few days.

## August Travel Tips: Navigating Peak Season Intelligently

### Choose Festivals Over Beaches

The Mediterranean in August is crowded and overpriced. Edinburgh, Kyoto's Obon, and the Hermanus Whale Festival are August-specific events that justify the high-season timing with experiences you simply cannot replicate in any other month. Build your August trip around an event, and the crowds become part of the experience rather than an annoyance.

### Consider the Southern Hemisphere

August is summer in your mind because of the Northern Hemisphere calendar. But flights to Buenos Aires, Cape Town, or Patagonia in August are cheaper than in their summer peak (November-February), accommodation costs less, and you get dramatic winter landscapes without the extreme cold of true polar winter. The knowledge gap between "August = summer" and "August = Southern Hemisphere winter escape" is your competitive advantage.

### The Monsoon Math

Southeast Asia's monsoon means different things in different places. Komodo and Flores are dry in August. Bali is mostly dry. Northern Thailand and Japan's south coast can be hit or miss. The rule: check specific regional data, not country-wide generalizations. A good rain pattern in one part of a country can be completely different 200km away.

## FAQ

### Is August a good month to travel?

August is peak season for Northern Hemisphere travel — the most popular, most expensive, and most crowded month in Mediterranean Europe. But it is also the month with the best festivals (Edinburgh Fringe, Kyoto's Obon), the peak of the Great Migration river crossings in Tanzania, dry-season perfection in Komodo and the Faroe Islands, and excellent Southern Hemisphere winter destinations like Patagonia and South Africa. August rewards travelers who plan around specific events and conditions rather than defaulting to beach destinations.

### Where is the cheapest place to travel in August?

Montenegro ($80-130/day) and Slovenia ($90-140/day) offer the best value in Europe. Patagonia can be surprisingly affordable in the Southern Hemisphere winter ($90-160/day). Costa Rica's green season brings prices down 30-50% from peak. [Komodo/Flores, Indonesia](/destinations/bali) runs $80-150/day. Avoid Santorini, Dubrovnik, and the Amalfi Coast — August is their most expensive month by far.

### Where should I go in August to avoid crowds?

The Faroe Islands offer dramatic scenery with minimal tourist infrastructure and few crowds. Slovenia's Lake Bled region is a fraction of the crowds of Croatia. South Africa's Hermanus sees mostly domestic visitors. Patagonia in winter is nearly deserted. And the Canadian Rockies — while popular — have private conservancy options in the adjacent wilderness that give you the same scenery with 90% fewer vehicles.

### Is August too hot for Mediterranean travel?

Parts of the Mediterranean are genuinely uncomfortable in August. Rome, Athens, and Barcelona regularly hit 35-38°C. The Algarve, the Adriatic coast, and the Greek islands remain manageable but are at peak crowding. If you go to the Mediterranean in August, choose coastal destinations over inland cities, book accommodation with air conditioning and a pool, plan sightseeing before 10 AM and after 5 PM, and budget 20-30% more than you would in June.

### What is the best destination for a first trip to Africa in August?

Tanzania's Serengeti offers the greatest wildlife spectacle on Earth in August — the Great Migration river crossings, when hundreds of thousands of wildebeest plunge across the crocodile-filled Mara River. It is not a budget safari, but nothing else compares as a first Africa experience. For a more affordable August Africa trip, South Africa's Western Cape — whale watching, wine, penguins, and Cape Town — delivers extraordinary variety at a price point most travelers can access.
