# The Most-Planned Destinations of 2026: What 1,500+ AI Trip Plans Reveal

Every "top destinations" list you read in January is built from the same two inputs: search volume and tourism-board press releases. Search data has a structural problem — it measures curiosity, not commitment. Googling a place costs three seconds. Building an itinerary with dates, a trip length, and the names of the four friends coming with you is a different act entirely, and it predicts actual travel far better.

We run an AI trip planner, so we can measure that second thing. Between May and July 2026 we analyzed 1,563 trip-planning sessions on MonkeyTravel — a 45-day window of real itineraries with real dates attached. [Tokyo](/destinations/tokyo) finishing first surprised nobody. Plenty of the rest did: [Taipei](/destinations/taipei) outplanned [Berlin](/destinations/berlin), one in five trips ran straight into our 14-day planning cap, and within a week of multi-city planning going live, a third of all saved trips chained two or more cities.

Here is what the data actually says.

## The 10 most-planned destinations of 2026

The top ten account for 42% of all 1,563 sessions. "Share" is the percentage of total planning sessions; "typical planned length" is the range most planners chose for that destination.

### 1. Tokyo — 161 sessions, and it isn't close

The yen has spent two years near multi-decade lows, which quietly deleted Japan's reputation as an expensive country: a proper bowl of ramen runs about $7, and a Tokyo business hotel often undercuts one in Lisbon. Japan logged a record 36.9 million visitors in 2024 and kept breaking monthly records through 2025, according to [JNTO](https://www.japan.travel/en/). Our data adds the part arrival statistics can't show: Tokyo planners think in routes. Tokyo–[Kyoto](/destinations/kyoto)–[Osaka](/destinations/osaka) was the single most-built city chain on the platform, and Tokyo leads among the 14-day trips. People aren't planning a city break — they're planning Japan.

### 2. Paris — the permanent number two

129 sessions, one in every twelve. [Paris](/destinations/paris) is the least surprising name on this list, but 2026 gives it two concrete tailwinds: Notre-Dame has been fully open since December 2024 — free, timed entry, on the order of 30,000 visitors a day — and hotel rates have settled back down after the 2024 Olympic premium. The 4–5 day typical length is the most classic city-break shape in our entire dataset.

### 3. London — sort the ETA before the theatre tickets

73 sessions. [London](/destinations/london)'s pull is structural: English-speaking, dense flight connections, free national museums, a theatre calendar that sells out months ahead. The 2026-specific detail planners keep missing is bureaucratic — nearly every visitor who doesn't need a visa now needs a [UK Electronic Travel Authorisation](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/apply-for-an-electronic-travel-authorisation-eta): £16, apply online before you fly. Budget ten minutes for it, and keep an eye on V&A East's new museum in Stratford, slated to open in 2026.

### 4. Bangkok — Southeast Asia's front door

55 sessions, and rarely alone on the itinerary. [Bangkok](/destinations/bangkok) is where long Southeast Asia trips begin: most Western passports currently get 60 visa-free days in Thailand (details at the [Tourism Authority of Thailand](https://www.tourismthailand.org/)), and the 7–10 day typical length says planners treat it as a hub — city first, then Chiang Mai or the islands. A plate of pad kaprao still costs about $2, which does more for Bangkok's ranking than any ad campaign ever will.

### 5. Rome — the post-Jubilee payoff

51 sessions. The 2025 Jubilee closed on January 6, 2026, after drawing north of 30 million pilgrims, and it left behind the most useful kind of souvenir: a renovated city. Piazza Pia pedestrianized, facades cleaned, transit upgraded. 2026 is the year [Rome](/destinations/rome) has Jubilee infrastructure without Jubilee crowds — our planners appear to have noticed before the airlines repriced it.

### 6. Barcelona — the tower finally tops out

41 sessions, with an asterisk worth reading: a noticeable slice of [Barcelona](/destinations/barcelona)'s sessions were planned in French — the direct TGV from Paris takes about six and a half hours, and it shows in the data. The 2026 hook is bigger. The Sagrada Família's central tower, the 172.5-meter Torre de Jesucrist, is slated for completion this year, a century after Gaudí's death ([official site](https://sagradafamilia.org/)). Barcelona–[Madrid](/destinations/madrid), 2.5 hours by AVE, also shows up repeatedly in our multi-city chains.

### 7. New York — a 250th birthday and a World Cup final

39 sessions. [New York](/destinations/new-york) has two calendar anchors in 2026: the United States' 250th anniversary on July 4, and the FIFA World Cup final at MetLife Stadium on July 19 — one of eight matches the New York–New Jersey area hosts. Expect hotel rates to spike around both dates; the 5–7 day plans in our data cluster on either side of them.

### 8. Bali — the two-week destination

38 sessions, and the clearest length signal in the top ten: [Bali](/destinations/bali) planners overwhelmingly built 10–14 day trips. A 13-plus-hour flight only makes sense amortized over two weeks, and our May–July window lines up with dry-season planning. One line item people still forget: the IDR 150,000 tourist levy (about $9), payable online before you land.

### 9. Taipei — the dark horse

38 sessions — tied with Bali, ahead of Berlin, and absent from every January listicle we checked. Two things are doing the work: night-market food at a third of Tokyo prices (stalls at Raohe and Ningxia carry Michelin Bib Gourmand nods), and a wave of new nonstop routes — EVA Air added Dallas–Fort Worth in late 2025, and Starlux opened Phoenix in early 2026. Taipei spent years as the connection city people flew through. In 2026 it's the destination.

### 10. Malta — the whole country in five days

25 sessions for the smallest country in the ranking. English is co-official, flights from most European hubs run under four hours, and Valletta, Mdina, Gozo and Comino all fit inside a single 5–7 day plan without a rental car racking up highway miles. Malta is what "short-haul that feels far" looks like in practice.

## Just outside the top ten

Berlin came in eleventh with 24 sessions, followed by a three-way tie at 19: [Budapest](/destinations/budapest), [Hong Kong](/destinations/hong-kong), and — the quiet signal in this dataset — Bergen. A Norwegian fjord city outplanning Vienna (13 sessions) in a summer window suggests the "coolcation", the heat-escape holiday, has moved from trend pieces into actual itineraries. Porto and Sydney logged 17 each, [Lisbon](/destinations/lisbon) 16, [Seoul](/destinations/seoul) 15, Hanoi 14, and Krakow, Melbourne, Dubrovnik and Vienna 13 apiece.

## One in five trips is exactly two weeks

The length distribution looks unremarkable at first: 5 days is the single most common trip, and 4–8 days is the fat middle where most itineraries live. Then there's the anomaly — a hard spike at 14 days. 326 sessions, 21% of everything we analyzed, planned exactly two weeks. Full disclosure: 14 days is currently the planning cap on MonkeyTravel, so that spike is demand pressing against a ceiling, and an unknown share of those planners wanted more. Either way the conclusion holds. The two-week trip — which a decade of fare unbundling and stingy leave policies squeezed toward extinction — is back in demand. Remote work makes it schedulable, and long-haul math makes it rational: a $900 fare to Tokyo amortizes far better over 14 nights than over six.

## A third of new trips chain cities

Within one week of our [multi-city trip planner](/multi-city-trip-planner) going live, 33% of all saved trips chained two or more cities. We expected a slow ramp. We got a third of the product's output, immediately. Real chains from that first stretch:

- Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka — the Golden Route, our most-built chain
- Athens–[Santorini](/destinations/santorini)–Mykonos
- Barcelona–Madrid
- Frankfurt–Heidelberg–Black Forest–Lauterbrunnen
- Boston–Salem–Portland
- Tbilisi–Telavi–Signagi
- Johor Bahru–Singapore

Look at the spread: a Japanese classic, a Greek island hop, a German-Swiss road trip, a New England coastal run, a Georgian wine route, a cross-border city pair. Multi-city isn't one demographic's habit. It's what trips look like once the tooling stops punishing you for adding a second stop.

## Two-thirds of trips are planned together

Of the sessions that specified a group type, 67% were group trips — couples, friends, families — against 33% solo. That ratio should change how you read travel advice, most of which is written as if one person with one opinion does the choosing. In a group trip the bottleneck isn't information, it's consensus: not "what are the best restaurants in Rome" but "which of these four does everyone accept." It's why we built voting and shared itineraries into the product, and it's why the group chat is where trips historically went to die. If you're the one who always ends up herding yours, [plan your trip](/trips/new) once and let people vote instead of screenshot-arguing.

## How we counted

The numbers come from 1,563 trip-planning sessions on MonkeyTravel across a 45-day window between May and July 2026. A session means someone built an itinerary for a destination, with a trip length and usually a group type. This is planning intent on one platform — not bookings, not arrivals, not global search volume. Our users skew European and plan in five languages, which is why European cities over-index here compared with US-centric rankings, and why French-language sessions helped push Barcelona up the table. We merged language variants of the same city before counting. The 14-day figure sits exactly at our planning cap, so read it as a floor on two-week demand, not a ceiling. And 45 days of early summer is a season, not a year — the January version of this table will look different, and we intend to publish it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the most planned destination for 2026?

Tokyo. It accounted for 161 of the 1,563 trip-planning sessions analyzed on MonkeyTravel between May and July 2026 — a 10.3% share, a 25% lead over Paris and more than double London. A yen near multi-decade lows and the Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka route habit explain most of the gap.

### How long are people planning trips in 2026?

Five days is the single most common trip length, and 4–8 days covers most itineraries. The outlier is the two-week trip: 21% of sessions — 326 of 1,563 — planned exactly 14 days, which is also the platform's current planning maximum, so real demand for long trips likely runs higher than that number shows.

### Are multi-city trips popular in 2026?

Yes, and the shift was abrupt: within one week of multi-city planning launching on MonkeyTravel, 33% of all saved trips chained two or more cities. The most-built combinations were Tokyo–Kyoto–Osaka, Athens–Santorini–Mykonos, and Barcelona–Madrid.
